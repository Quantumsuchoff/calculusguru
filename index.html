Geometric Series — Rigorous SOP

The Reindexing Transformation: a mechanical protocol that maps any starting index $k$ to the canonical $m=0$ form. Follow this once and you'll never need to memorize special cases.

I.
The Fundamental Identity
Everything reduces to this
Core Identity

Every geometric series, regardless of starting index, reduces to the same closed form via the Reindexing Transformation:

$$\sum_{n=k}^{\infty}ar^{n}=\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}ar^{m+k}=ar^{k}\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}r^m=\frac{ar^k}{1-r}$$

Whether the series has nested exponents, negative ratios, or non-standard starting points — this identity is the final destination. The protocol below is the guaranteed path to get there.

Φ
The Rigorous Geometric Series Protocol (RGSP)
Six deterministic steps — no intuition required
Step 1 — Domain & Convergence Guardrail

Before any algebra, verify the series is geometric and converges.

  • ①Geometricity Test: Verify $\frac{u_{n+1}}{u_n} = r$, where $r$ is constant and independent of $n$.
  • ②Convergence Criterion: If $|r| \geq 1$, the series diverges by the $n$-th Term Test. Stop. If $|r| < 1$, proceed.
Step 2 — Decomposition (Linearity)

If the summand is a sum or difference of multiple terms, split it:

$$\sum (u_n \pm v_n) = \sum u_n \pm \sum v_n$$

Evaluate each resulting series independently. If one part diverges, the entire sum diverges.

Step 3 — Canonical Normalization

Rewrite the series so $n$ appears only as a clean, positive power. Use the exponent laws to isolate the base $r$:

$$r^{-n} \to \left(\tfrac{1}{r}\right)^n \qquad r^{n+p} \to r^p \cdot r^n \qquad \frac{x^n}{y^n} \to \left(\frac{x}{y}\right)^n$$

Target form: $\displaystyle\sum_{n=k}^{\infty} a \cdot r^n$

Step 4 — Parametric Extraction

Extract the three required parameters:

$$a = \text{constant coefficient} \qquad r = \text{common ratio (base)} \qquad k = \text{starting index}$$
Step 5 — The Reindexing Transformation (The Footwork)

Apply the substitution $m = n - k$ to shift the starting index to zero:

$$\sum_{n=k}^{\infty} ar^n = \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} ar^{m+k} = ar^k \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} r^m$$
Step 6 — Evaluation via Geometric Identity

Replace the infinite sum with its closed-form value:

$$\boxed{\text{Sum} = ar^k \cdot \frac{1}{1-r} = \frac{ar^k}{1-r}}$$
∑
Worked Examples
The protocol applied step-by-step
1
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=3}^{\infty} \frac{5 \cdot 2^{n-1}}{3^{n+1}}$
Step 1 — Convergence

Ratio $r = 2/3$. Since $|2/3| < 1$, the series converges. Proceed.

Step 3 — Normalization
$$\frac{5 \cdot 2^n \cdot 2^{-1}}{3^n \cdot 3^1} = \frac{5 \cdot \frac{1}{2}}{3} \cdot \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^n = \frac{5}{6} \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^n$$
Step 4 — Extraction
$$a = \tfrac{5}{6}, \qquad r = \tfrac{2}{3}, \qquad k = 3$$
Steps 5 & 6 — Reindex & Evaluate
$$\frac{5}{6} \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^3 \cdot \frac{1}{1 - 2/3} = \frac{5}{6} \cdot \frac{8}{27} \cdot 3 = \frac{20}{27}$$
Result
$$\boxed{\frac{20}{27}}$$
2
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} 2x(x^2)^n$ — Variable Ratio
Step 1 — Convergence

Ratio $r = x^2$. Converges only if $|x^2| < 1$, i.e. $|x| < 1$. Outside this interval: diverges.

Step 4 — Extraction
$$a = 2x, \qquad r = x^2, \qquad k = 1$$
Steps 5 & 6 — Reindex & Evaluate
$$\sum_{m=0}^{\infty} 2x(x^2)^{m+1} = 2x(x^2)^1 \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} (x^2)^m = 2x^3 \cdot \frac{1}{1-x^2}$$
Result (for $|x| < 1$)
$$\boxed{\frac{2x^3}{1-x^2}}$$
3
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{3^{n-1} - 2}{4^{n+1}}$ — Linearity Required
Step 2 — Decomposition
$$\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{3^{n-1}}{4^{n+1}} - \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{2}{4^{n+1}}$$
Series 1 Normalized
$$\frac{3^{n-1}}{4^{n+1}} = \frac{1}{12}\left(\frac{3}{4}\right)^n \implies a=\tfrac{1}{12},\; r=\tfrac{3}{4},\; k=2$$
$$\text{Sum}_1 = \frac{1}{12}\cdot\frac{9}{16}\cdot\frac{1}{1/4} = \frac{3}{16}$$
Series 2 Normalized
$$\frac{2}{4^{n+1}} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{4}\right)^n \implies a=\tfrac{1}{2},\; r=\tfrac{1}{4},\; k=2$$
$$\text{Sum}_2 = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{16}\cdot\frac{4}{3} = \frac{1}{24}$$
Result
$$\frac{3}{16} - \frac{1}{24} = \frac{9}{48} - \frac{2}{48} = \boxed{\frac{7}{48}}$$
Geometric Series SOP — Rigorous Geometric Series Protocol (RGSP)
Every geometric sum reduces to: $\dfrac{ar^k}{1-r}$ for $|r| < 1$.

Alternating Series — Rigorous ASP

Leibniz's Criteria formalized into a tiered diagnostic. Classifies every alternating series as Absolutely Convergent, Conditionally Convergent, or Divergent.

I.
Leibniz's Criteria (The Alternating Series Test)
The two conditions — both must hold
Form

A series of the form $\sum_{n=k}^{\infty} (-1)^n b_n$ or $(-1)^{n+1} b_n$ converges if and only if both conditions hold:

$$\textbf{1. Monotonicity:}\quad b_{n+1} \leq b_n \;\;\forall\, n \geq K \qquad \textbf{2. Vanishing Limit:}\quad \lim_{n\to\infty} b_n = 0$$

If either fails — especially if the limit is nonzero — the series diverges by the $n$-th Term Test.

Key Insight — Absolute vs. Conditional

Absolutely Convergent: $\sum |u_n|$ converges. The series is so strong it doesn't need the alternating signs. If the Ratio Test yields $L < 1$: done.

Conditionally Convergent: $\sum |u_n|$ diverges, but $\sum u_n$ converges via AST. The alternating signs alone rescued it.

If the Ratio Test gives $L < 1$: Absolute Convergence — ignore the AST entirely.

Φ
Rigorous Alternating Series Protocol (RASP v2.0)
Five phases — handles all standard cases and edge cases
Phase 1 — Anatomy & Oscillator Check
  • ①The Alternator: Strip $(-1)^n$, $(-1)^{n+1}$, $\cos(n\pi)$ from the summand.
  • ②The Positive Sequence ($b_n$): What remains. Ensure $b_n > 0$ for all $n \geq k$.
  • ③Irregular Oscillator Check: If the oscillator is $\sin(n)$ or $\cos(n)$ (signs don't flip every term) — attempt Phase 2, then escalate to Dirichlet's Test if Phase 2 fails.
Phase 2 — Absolute Bypass (Priority 1)

Evaluate $\sum |u_n| = \sum b_n$ using positive-term tests. Use the Ratio Test if you see $n!$ or constants raised to $n$.

$L=1$ or $\sum b_n$ diverges (harmonic, $p\leq1$)inconclusiveProceed to Phase 3.
Phase 3 — Zero-Limit Guardrail

Evaluate $\lim_{n\to\infty} b_n$.

ResultVerdict

Phase 4 — Monotonicity Filter

Show $b_{n+1} \leq b_n$ for all $n$ beyond some point $K$. Three methods:

  • AAlgebraic: Constant numerator, growing denominator → trivially decreasing.
  • BRatio Method: Show $\frac{b_{n+1}}{b_n} \leq 1$.
  • CCalculus Method: Let $f(x)=b_x$. If $f'(x) < 0$ for $x \geq k$, the sequence is decreasing.

If the sequence approaches zero but "wobbles" (non-monotonic): AST is inconclusive — escalate to Dirichlet's Test.

Known Edge Cases — Where the AST Breaks
Know the limits of the protocol
Edge Case 1 — The "Wobbly" Shrink

Suppose $b_n = 1/n$ for odd $n$ and $b_n = 1/n^2$ for even $n$. The limit is zero, but the sequence jumps between patterns and isn't strictly decreasing every step. Phase 4 is inconclusive. The RASP hits a wall — advanced bounding techniques are needed.

Edge Case 2 — The "Imperfect" Alternator ($\sin n$)

The series $\sum \sin(n)/n$ oscillates but not predictably — signs may go $+\,+\,-\,+\,-\,-$. The AST requires perfect destructive interference ($+ - + - \ldots$), which $\sin(n)$ does not guarantee. Phase 1 routes you to Phase 2 only; if Phase 2 also fails, you must escalate to Dirichlet's Test.

Alternating Series SOP — RASP v2.0
Bypass Absolute → Zero Limit → Monotonicity → Classify.

Direct Comparison Test — DCT SOP

The analytical equivalent of bounding a suspect variable. Demand strict absolute inequalities. The tool of choice when the series contains bounded functions — sines, cosines, arctangents, logarithms.

Φ
Direct Comparison Test Protocol
Four phases — from bounding to conclusion
Phase 1 — Bounding Assessment

Identify components that can be strictly bounded by known constants:

FunctionBound
$\sin(n)$ or $\cos(n)$$-1 \leq \sin n \leq 1$; $\;\;0 \leq \sin^2 n \leq 1$
$\arctan(n)$ for $n > 0$$0 < \arctan n < \pi/2$
$\ln(n)$ for $n \geq 3$$\ln n > 1$; $\;\;\ln n < n$ for all $n \geq 1$
Additive constants in denominatorControls whether fraction grows or shrinks when removed
Phase 2 — Inequality Pivot

Goal: Convergence (create a ceiling) → make $a_n$ larger: $a_n \leq b_n$

  • ↑Replace bounded numerator with its maximum value.
  • ↑Remove positive additive terms from denominator (smaller denominator = larger fraction).

Goal: Divergence (create a floor) → make $a_n$ smaller: $a_n \geq b_n$

  • ↓Replace bounded numerator with its minimum value.
  • ↓Remove subtracted terms from denominator (larger denominator = smaller fraction).
Phase 3 — Target Evaluation

Evaluate the simplified comparison series $b_n$ — usually a $p$-series $\sum 1/n^p$ or geometric series.

Phase 4 — Strict Conclusion

Worked Examples
Five canonical DCT problems
1
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2+5}$
Pivot

Removing $+5$ shrinks the denominator, making the fraction larger: $\;\frac{1}{n^2+5} < \frac{1}{n^2}$

Target

$b_n = 1/n^2$ — convergent $p$-series ($p=2>1$).

Conclusion

Smaller than a convergent series → CONVERGES

2
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{\sin^2(n)}{n^3}$
Pivot

$\sin^2(n) \leq 1$ always, so: $\;\frac{\sin^2(n)}{n^3} \leq \frac{1}{n^3}$

Conclusion

$b_n = 1/n^3$ converges ($p=3$) → CONVERGES

3
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{2+\cos(n)}{n}$
Pivot

$\cos(n) \geq -1$ always, so $2+\cos(n) \geq 1$, giving: $\;\frac{2+\cos(n)}{n} \geq \frac{1}{n}$

Conclusion

$b_n = 1/n$ is the harmonic series (diverges) → DIVERGES

4
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{\ln(n)}{n}$
Pivot

For $n \geq 3$: $\ln(n) > 1$, so $\;\frac{\ln(n)}{n} > \frac{1}{n}$

Conclusion

Larger than the divergent harmonic series → DIVERGES

5
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=2}^{\infty}\frac{\arctan(n)}{n-1}$
Double Pivot

For $n\geq2$: $\arctan(n) > \arctan(1) = \pi/4$, and $n-1 < n$, so:

$$\frac{\arctan(n)}{n-1} > \frac{\pi/4}{n}$$
Conclusion

$(\pi/4)\cdot(1/n)$ is a constant multiple of the harmonic series → DIVERGES

Direct Comparison Test SOP — Bound → Pivot → Target → Conclude.
DCT shines when bounded functions (trig, log, arctan) make limits impossible to compute.

Limit Comparison Test — LCT SOP

Identify a "prototype" series that mirrors $a_n$ as $n\to\infty$. If their growth rates are proportional, their convergence behaviors are identical.

Φ
Limit Comparison Test Protocol
Four phases — dominant terms to conclusion
Phase 1 — Dominant Term Inspection

Use LCT when the summand is a rational function (polynomial/polynomial) or involves radicals ($\sqrt{n}$). At infinity, lower-order terms vanish — the series "acts like" its leading terms. Identify the highest power of $n$ in the numerator and denominator.

Phase 2 — Construction of Comparison Series ($b_n$)
$$b_n = \frac{n^{\text{top power}}}{n^{\text{bottom power}}} = \frac{1}{n^p}$$

Simplify $b_n$ into $p$-series form $1/n^p$ to enable easy evaluation.

Phase 3 — Limit Evaluation (The Footwork)
$$L = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n}$$

If $0 < L < \infty$ (finite and positive): both series behave identically — they share the same fate.

Phase 4 — Conclusion by $p$-series
$p$ value$b_n$ verdict$a_n$ verdict
$p > 1$ConvergesCONVERGES
$p \leq 1$DivergesDIVERGES

LCT vs. DCT — The Decision Pipeline
Run this logic gate before picking your test
Triggers for LCT
  • →Messy Polynomials: Fractions with multiple powers of $n$, like $\sum \frac{3n^2-5n+2}{7n^5+n}$.
  • →Radicals with Polynomials: Square roots mixed with powers, like $\sum \frac{1}{\sqrt{n^3-2}}$.
  • →DCT Inequality Fails: Example — to prove $\sum\frac{1}{n^2-1}$ converges, you'd compare to $1/n^2$, but $\frac{1}{n^2-1} > \frac{1}{n^2}$, ruining the ceiling. DCT is dead here. LCT handles it since it only needs the limit of the ratio.
Triggers for DCT
  • →Trigonometry: $\sin n$, $\cos n$, $\arctan n$. LCT fails because $\lim \sin n$ doesn't exist. DCT immediately replaces $\sin^2 n$ with $1$.
  • →Logarithms: $\ln n$ lacks a clean polynomial equivalent. DCT bounds it: $\ln n < n$ or $\ln n > 1$.
  • →Clean Additive Constants: $\frac{1}{n^3+5}$ — just drop $+5$ to get the ceiling. DCT is faster than the LCT limit here.
Quick Decision Logic
  • 1Does it have $\sin$, $\cos$, $\arctan$, or $\ln$? → Use DCT.
  • 2Is it a fraction of polynomials or roots? → Use LCT.
  • 3Denominator has a minus sign (like $n^3-4$) making DCT ceiling wrong? → Use LCT.

LCT applied to three canonical forms

$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{n+1}{n^2+n+1}$

Top: $n^1$. Bottom: $n^2$. So $b_n = 1/n$ — divergent harmonic series.

$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{n^2+1}}$

Limit
$$L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n}{\sqrt{n^2+1}} = \sqrt{\lim\frac{n^2}{n^2+1}} = 1$$
Conclusion

DIVERGES

$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{5n^2+1}{2n^4+3n}$

Root Test — SOPRT

The optimal tool when the entire expression is raised to the $n$-th power. The $n$-th root strips the exponent and exposes the base — then the limit tells all.

I.
Formal Root Test Criteria
When to use it and what $L$ means
The Test

Given $\sum a_n$, compute:

$$L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\sqrt[n]{|a_n|} = \lim_{n\to\infty}|a_n|^{1/n}$$
$L$ valueVerdict
$L < 1$ABSOLUTELY CONVERGENT
$L > 1$ or $L = \infty$DIVERGENT
$L = 1$INCONCLUSIVE — use another test
When to Choose the Root Test

Use the Root Test when the general term is power-dominant:

$$a_n = [f(n)]^n \qquad a_n = [f(n)]^{n^2} \qquad a_n = \frac{c^n}{g(n)^n}$$

If the term contains factorials ($n!$), the Ratio Test is usually more efficient — the Root Test struggles with factorials.

Φ
Root Test Protocol (SOPRT)
Five phases — identification to characterization
Phase 1 — Identification

Verify the general term $a_n$ has a functional base raised to a power involving $n$. Confirm the Root Test applies.

Phase 2 — Radical Transformation
$$\sqrt[n]{|a_n|} = (|a_n|)^{1/n}$$
Phase 3 — Algebraic Simplification

Apply $(x^a)^b = x^{ab}$ to eliminate the outer power:

$$\text{If } a_n = [f(n)]^n:\quad ([f(n)]^n)^{1/n} = |f(n)|$$
$$\text{If } a_n = [f(n)]^{n^2}:\quad ([f(n)]^{n^2})^{1/n} = |f(n)|^n$$
Phase 4 — Limit Evaluation

Evaluate $L = \lim_{n\to\infty}|a_n|^{1/n}$. Use L'Hôpital's Rule if an indeterminate form arises.

Phase 5 — Final Characterization

Compare $L$ to the unit threshold and state the verdict.

Worked Examples
Three power-dominant series
1
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left(\frac{2n}{3n+1}\right)^n$
Transform & Simplify
$$L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left[\left(\frac{2n}{3n+1}\right)^n\right]^{1/n} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2n}{3n+1} = \frac{2}{3}$$
Conclusion

$L = 2/3 < 1$ → CONVERGES

2
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{(\ln n)^n}$
Transform & Simplify
$$a_n = \left(\frac{1}{\ln n}\right)^n \implies L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{\ln n} = 0$$
Conclusion

$L = 0 < 1$ → CONVERGES

3
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left(\frac{7}{2^n}+\frac{3}{5}\right)^{n^2}$ — Higher Power
Transform & Simplify
$$L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left[\left(\frac{7}{2^n}+\frac{3}{5}\right)^{n^2}\right]^{1/n} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left(\frac{7}{2^n}+\frac{3}{5}\right)^n$$

As $n\to\infty$: $\frac{7}{2^n}\to 0$, so the limit becomes $\lim_{n\to\infty}\left(\frac{3}{5}\right)^n = 0$.

Conclusion

$L = 0 < 1$ → CONVERGES

Root Test SOP — Identify → Transform → Simplify → Evaluate $L$ → Characterize.
Use when the entire expression is raised to the $n$-th power. Avoid for factorials.

Telescoping Series — Convergence SOP

Internal terms cancel — like a collapsing telescope. These are rare series where the exact value of the sum can be computed with precision, not just classified.

I.
The Telescoping Structure
Why almost everything cancels
Core Mechanism

If $a_n = b_n - b_{n+1}$, the partial sum $S_k$ expands into a chain where everything in the middle cancels:

$$S_k = (b_1 - b_2) + (b_2 - b_3) + (b_3 - b_4) + \cdots + (b_k - b_{k+1}) = b_1 - b_{k+1}$$

Convergence then hinges entirely on the "tail" $b_{k+1}$: if $\lim_{k\to\infty} b_{k+1}$ is finite, the series converges to $b_1 - \lim b_{k+1}$.

Φ
Telescoping Series Protocol
Five phases — identify to exact value
Phase 1 — Identification & Inspection

Determine if the series can be written in the form $(b_n - b_{n+k})$. Look for:

  • →Subtraction between similar functional forms
  • →Rational functions with factorable quadratic denominators → Partial Fraction Decomposition
  • →Logarithmic form: $\ln(n+1) - \ln(n)$ via $\ln(A/B) = \ln A - \ln B$
Phase 2 — Partial Fraction Decomposition (if needed)

If $a_n$ is rational, decompose into $\frac{A}{f(n)} - \frac{B}{g(n)}$. Solve for $A$ and $B$ by setting up the covering equation and evaluating at strategic values of $n$.

Phase 3 — Partial Sum Expansion ($S_k$)

Write out the first 3–4 terms and the last 2 terms of $S_k$ explicitly. This makes the cancellation pattern visible.

Phase 4 — Cancellation & Reduction

Strike through the cancelling terms. If the "gap" between terms is $>1$ (e.g., $b_n - b_{n+2}$), more than one term will survive at each end.

Phase 5 — Limit Evaluation
$$S = \lim_{k\to\infty} S_k$$

If the limit is finite → converges to that value. If the limit is $\pm\infty$ → diverges.

↔
Worked Examples
Six canonical telescoping problems
1
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\!\left(\frac{1}{n} - \frac{1}{n+1}\right)$
Already telescoping

$S_k = (1-\tfrac{1}{2})+(\tfrac{1}{2}-\tfrac{1}{3})+\cdots+(\tfrac{1}{k}-\tfrac{1}{k+1}) = 1 - \tfrac{1}{k+1}$

Result

$S = \lim_{k\to\infty}(1 - \tfrac{1}{k+1}) = \mathbf{1}$

2
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^2+n}$
Partial Fractions

$\frac{1}{n(n+1)} = \frac{1}{n} - \frac{1}{n+1}$. Identical to Example 1.

Result

$S = \mathbf{1}$

3
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\ln\!\left(\frac{n+1}{n}\right)$ — Divergent
Log Rule

$\ln\tfrac{n+1}{n} = \ln(n+1) - \ln n$. Expansion: $S_k = \ln(k+1) - \ln 1 = \ln(k+1)$.

Result

$S = \lim \ln(k+1) = \infty$ → DIVERGES

4
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\!\left(\cos\tfrac{1}{n} - \cos\tfrac{1}{n+1}\right)$
Reduction

$S_k = \cos 1 - \cos\tfrac{1}{k+1}$

Result

$S = \cos 1 - \cos 0 = \cos(1) - 1$

5
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\!\left(\frac{1}{e^n} - \frac{1}{e^{n+1}}\right)$
Reduction

$S_k = \tfrac{1}{e} - \tfrac{1}{e^{k+1}}$

Result

$S = \dfrac{1}{e}$

6
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^2+3n+2}$
Partial Fractions

$(n+1)(n+2)$ → $\frac{1}{n+1}-\frac{1}{n+2}$. $S_k = \tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{1}{k+2}$.

Result

$S = \dfrac{1}{2}$

Telescoping Series SOP — Identify → Decompose → Expand $S_k$ → Cancel → Evaluate limit.
These are the only series where the exact sum, not just convergence, can be found directly.
