Geometric Series — Rigorous SOP

The Reindexing Transformation maps any starting index $k$ to the canonical $m=0$ form. Follow this once and you'll never memorize special cases again.

I.
The Fundamental Identity
Everything reduces to this
Core Identity

Every geometric series, regardless of starting index, reduces to the same closed form via the Reindexing Transformation:

$$\sum_{n=k}^{\infty}ar^{n}=\frac{ar^k}{1-r}, \quad |r|<1$$
Φ
Rigorous Geometric Series Protocol (RGSP)
Six deterministic steps — no intuition required
Step 1 — Convergence Guardrail

Verify geometricity: $u_{n+1}/u_n = r$ (constant). If $|r| \geq 1$, DIVERGES by $n$-th Term Test. If $|r| < 1$, proceed.

Step 2 — Decomposition (Linearity)
$$\sum (u_n \pm v_n) = \sum u_n \pm \sum v_n$$

Evaluate each independently. If one diverges, the whole sum diverges.

Step 3 — Canonical Normalization
$$r^{-n} \to \left(\tfrac{1}{r}\right)^n \qquad r^{n+p} \to r^p \cdot r^n \qquad \frac{x^n}{y^n} \to \left(\frac{x}{y}\right)^n$$

Target form: $\displaystyle\sum_{n=k}^{\infty} a \cdot r^n$

Step 4 — Parametric Extraction
$$a = \text{constant coefficient} \qquad r = \text{common ratio} \qquad k = \text{starting index}$$
Step 5 — Reindexing Transformation
$$\sum_{n=k}^{\infty} ar^n = ar^k \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} r^m$$
Step 6 — Evaluation
$$\boxed{\text{Sum} = \frac{ar^k}{1-r}}$$
∑
Worked Examples
The protocol applied step-by-step
1
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=3}^{\infty} \frac{5 \cdot 2^{n-1}}{3^{n+1}}$
Normalization
$$\frac{5 \cdot 2^n \cdot 2^{-1}}{3^n \cdot 3} = \frac{5}{6}\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^n \implies a=\tfrac{5}{6},\;r=\tfrac{2}{3},\;k=3$$
Result
$$\frac{5}{6}\cdot\frac{(2/3)^3}{1-2/3}=\frac{5}{6}\cdot\frac{8}{27}\cdot 3=\boxed{\frac{20}{27}}$$
2
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{3^{n-1} - 2}{4^{n+1}}$ — Linearity Required
Decomposition
$$\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{3^{n-1}}{4^{n+1}} - \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{2}{4^{n+1}}$$
Series 1: $a=\tfrac{1}{12},r=\tfrac{3}{4},k=2$
$$\text{Sum}_1 = \tfrac{1}{12}\cdot\tfrac{9}{16}\cdot 4 = \tfrac{3}{16}$$
Series 2: $a=\tfrac{1}{2},r=\tfrac{1}{4},k=2$
$$\text{Sum}_2 = \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot\tfrac{1}{16}\cdot\tfrac{4}{3} = \tfrac{1}{24}$$
Result
$$\frac{3}{16}-\frac{1}{24}=\frac{9}{48}-\frac{2}{48}=\boxed{\frac{7}{48}}$$

Every geometric sum reduces to $\dfrac{ar^k}{1-r}$ for $|r|<1$.

Alternating Series — Rigorous ASP

Leibniz's Criteria formalized into a tiered diagnostic. Classifies every alternating series as Absolutely Convergent, Conditionally Convergent, or Divergent.

I.
Leibniz's Criteria
Both conditions must hold simultaneously
Form

A series $\sum (-1)^n b_n$ converges if and only if both hold:

$$\textbf{1. Monotonicity:}\; b_{n+1}\leq b_n \;\;\textbf{2. Vanishing:}\; \lim_{n\to\infty}b_n=0$$
Absolute vs. Conditional

Absolutely Convergent: $\sum |u_n|$ converges — signs are irrelevant.
Conditionally Convergent: $\sum |u_n|$ diverges, but $\sum u_n$ converges via AST.

Φ
RASP v2.0 — Five Phases
Handles all standard cases and edge cases
Phase 1 — Anatomy

Strip $(-1)^n$ or $(-1)^{n+1}$. The positive remainder is $b_n$. If oscillator is $\sin n$ or $\cos n$: attempt Phase 2, then escalate to Dirichlet's Test.

Phase 2 — Absolute Bypass
OutcomeVerdict
Ratio/Root Test $L<1$ABSOLUTELY CONVERGENT
Ratio/Root Test $L>1$DIVERGENT
$L=1$ or $\sum b_n$ divergesinconclusive → Phase 3
Phase 3 — Zero-Limit Guardrail

If $\lim b_n \neq 0$: DIVERGES. If $\lim b_n = 0$: proceed to Phase 4.

Phase 4 — Monotonicity Filter
  • AAlgebraic: Constant numerator, growing denominator.
  • BRatio: Show $b_{n+1}/b_n \leq 1$.
  • CCalculus: $f'(x) < 0$ for $x\geq k$.

The analytical equivalent of bounding a suspect variable. The tool of choice when the series contains bounded functions — sines, cosines, arctangents, logarithms.

Telescoping Series — Convergence SOP

Internal terms cancel — like a collapsing telescope. These are rare series where the exact sum can be computed with precision, not just classified.

I.
The Telescoping Structure
Why almost everything cancels
Core Mechanism

If $a_n=b_n-b_{n+1}$, the partial sum collapses:

$$S_k=(b_1-b_2)+(b_2-b_3)+\cdots+(b_k-b_{k+1})=b_1-b_{k+1}$$

Series converges to $b_1-\lim_{k\to\infty}b_{k+1}$ if that limit is finite.

Φ
Telescoping Protocol — Five Phases
Identify to exact value
Phase 1 — Identification
  • →Subtraction between shifted functional forms
  • →Rational with factorable denominator → Partial Fractions
  • →Logarithmic: $\ln\frac{n+1}{n}=\ln(n+1)-\ln n$
Phase 2 — Partial Fractions (if needed)

Decompose $a_n$ into $\frac{A}{f(n)}-\frac{B}{g(n)}$.

Phase 3 — Partial Sum $S_k$

Write first 3–4 and last 2 terms explicitly to reveal the cancellation pattern.

Phase 4 — Cancel and Reduce

Strike the interior terms. Gaps $>1$ leave more survivors at each end.

Phase 5 — Limit
$$S=\lim_{k\to\infty}S_k$$
↔

Cauchy Condensation Test

For slowly-decaying series where $p$-series comparisons fail. Compresses an infinite series into a geometrically-sampled counterpart that is usually much easier to classify.

I.
The Condensation Theorem
The biconditional that drives everything
Theorem (Cauchy, 1821)

Let $\{a_n\}$ be a non-increasing, non-negative sequence. Then:

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_n \;\text{ converges}\iff\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}2^n a_{2^n}\;\text{ converges}$$

The condensed series $\sum 2^n a_{2^n}$ samples $a_n$ only at powers of 2. Because the original sequence is non-increasing, the skipped terms are bounded between consecutive sampled values — the total behavior is preserved.

Why This Works — Intuition

Group the original series into dyadic blocks: $\{a_1\}$, $\{a_2, a_3\}$, $\{a_4,\ldots,a_7\}$, $\{a_8,\ldots,a_{15}\}$, …

Block $k$ has $2^{k-1}$ terms, all between $a_{2^{k-1}}$ and $a_{2^k}$. So each block is squeezed between $2^{k-1}a_{2^k}$ and $2^{k-1}a_{2^{k-1}}$. Summing these bounds gives the biconditional.

Φ
Cauchy Condensation Protocol
Five deterministic steps
Step 1 — Verify Applicability

Confirm $\{a_n\}$ is non-increasing ($a_{n+1}\leq a_n$) and non-negative ($a_n\geq 0$). If not monotone, the test does not apply — consider DCT or LCT instead.

Step 2 — Form the Condensed Series

Replace $n$ with $2^n$ throughout $a_n$, then multiply by $2^n$:

$$\text{Condensed series:}\quad\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}2^n\cdot a_{2^n}$$
Step 3 — Simplify the Condensed Term

Apply exponent laws to simplify $2^n\cdot a_{2^n}$. The result is typically a geometric series or $p$-series — far simpler than the original.

Step 4 — Classify the Condensed Series

Use any applicable test on the simplified condensed series.

Step 5 — Apply the Biconditional

The original and condensed series always agree: both converge or both diverge. State the conclusion for $\sum a_n$.

2ⁿ
Worked Examples
Logarithmic and iterated-log series — where CCT shines
1
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=2}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n\ln n}$ — The Prototype
Applicability Check

$a_n=\frac{1}{n\ln n}$: denominator grows → $a_n$ is positive and non-increasing. ✓

Condense
$$2^n\cdot a_{2^n}=2^n\cdot\frac{1}{2^n\ln(2^n)}=\frac{1}{n\ln 2}$$
Classify Condensed Series
$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n\ln 2}=\frac{1}{\ln 2}\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n}=\frac{1}{\ln 2}\cdot\infty$$

The condensed series is a constant multiple of the harmonic series — it diverges.

Conclusion

$\displaystyle\sum\frac{1}{n\ln n}$ — DIVERGES

2
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=2}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n(\ln n)^2}$
Condense
$$2^n\cdot a_{2^n}=2^n\cdot\frac{1}{2^n(n\ln 2)^2}=\frac{1}{n^2(\ln 2)^2}$$
Classify

$\displaystyle\frac{1}{(\ln 2)^2}\sum\frac{1}{n^2}$ — convergent $p$-series ($p=2>1$).

Conclusion

$\displaystyle\sum\frac{1}{n(\ln n)^2}$ — CONVERGES

3
$\displaystyle\sum\frac{1}{n^p}$ — CCT Recovers the $p$-Series Test
Condense
$$2^n\cdot(2^n)^{-p}=2^n\cdot 2^{-np}=2^{n(1-p)}=(2^{1-p})^n$$
Classify

Geometric series with ratio $r=2^{1-p}$. Converges iff $2^{1-p}<1$, i.e., $1-p<0$, i.e., $p>1$.

Conclusion

CCT independently proves: converges iff $p>1$. ✓

Olivier's Test — Necessary Condition

A necessary (not sufficient) condition for convergence of monotone series. Its power lies in the contrapositive: if $na_n\not\to 0$, divergence is immediate — no further work needed.

I.
Olivier's Theorem
The theorem and its critical limitations
Theorem (Olivier, 1827)

Let $\{a_n\}$ be a positive, non-increasing sequence. If $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_n$ converges, then:

$$\lim_{n\to\infty}n\cdot a_n=0$$

Equivalently (contrapositive): if $\lim_{n\to\infty}na_n\neq 0$, then the series diverges.

CRITICAL — This Is NOT a Sufficient Condition

The converse is FALSE. The condition $na_n\to 0$ does not imply convergence.

Counterexample: $a_n=\frac{1}{n\ln n}$. Then $na_n=\frac{1}{\ln n}\to 0$. But $\sum\frac{1}{n\ln n}$ diverges (by Cauchy Condensation).

Olivier's test can only confirm divergence via the contrapositive — it can never confirm convergence.

Φ
Olivier's Test Protocol
Four phases — one-way gate to divergence
Phase 1 — Applicability Check

Verify $a_n>0$ and $\{a_n\}$ is non-increasing. If the sequence oscillates or is not monotone, Olivier's Test does not apply.

Phase 2 — Compute $na_n$

Multiply the general term $a_n$ by $n$. Simplify fully.

Phase 3 — Evaluate the Limit
$$\lim_{n\to\infty}n\cdot a_n=L$$
Phase 4 — Conclusion
Limit $L$Verdict
$L\neq 0$ (including $L=\infty$)DIVERGES (contrapositive)
$L=0$INCONCLUSIVE — escalate to another test
Ω

3

Compute $na_n$
$$n\cdot\frac{1}{n\ln n}=\frac{1}{\ln n}\to 0$$

Olivier's says INCONCLUSIVE. But this series diverges (by Cauchy Condensation). This is the prime example of why $na_n\to 0$ cannot imply convergence.

Conclusion

Olivier's: INCONCLUSIVE. Must use Cauchy Condensation → DIVERGES.

Dirichlet's Test

The supreme tool for series of the form $\sum a_n b_n$ where $b_n$ oscillates with bounded partial sums and $a_n$ decays monotonically to zero. Subsumes the Alternating Series Test as a special case.

I.
Dirichlet's Test — Formal Statement
Two conditions, one powerful conclusion
Theorem (Dirichlet)

Suppose both of the following hold:

  • ①$\{a_n\}$ decays: $a_n$ is positive, non-increasing, and $\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n=0$.
  • ②$b_n$ has bounded partial sums: $\exists\, M>0$ such that $\left|\sum_{n=1}^{k}b_n\right|\leq M$ for all $k$.

Then $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_n b_n$ converges.

Connection to the Alternating Series Test

The AST is Dirichlet's Test with $b_n=(-1)^n$. The partial sums of $(-1)^n$ alternate between $-1$ and $0$ — always bounded by $M=1$. Then $a_n\to 0$ monotonically gives convergence. Dirichlet's Test is strictly more general: $b_n$ need not alternate perfectly.

Φ
Dirichlet's Test Protocol
Five phases — decomposition to convergence
Phase 1 — Identification

Recognize the summand as a product $a_n b_n$. Look for a decaying factor ($1/n$, $1/n^p$, $1/\ln n$) and an oscillating factor ($\sin n$, $\cos n$, $(-1)^n$, $e^{in\theta}$).

Phase 2 — Decompose and Label

Assign the decaying factor to $a_n$ and the oscillating factor to $b_n$.

Phase 3 — Verify $a_n$ Decays

Confirm $a_n>0$, non-increasing, and $a_n\to 0$. Use derivatives or algebraic comparison.

Phase 4 — Bound the Partial Sums of $b_n$

Show $\left|\sum_{n=1}^k b_n\right|\leq M$ for all $k$. For $\sin(n\theta)$ and $\cos(n\theta)$, use the closed form:

$$\left|\sum_{n=1}^k\sin(n\theta)\right|\leq\frac{1}{|\sin(\theta/2)|}, \quad\theta\neq 2m\pi$$
Phase 5 — Conclude

Both conditions verified → $\sum a_n b_n$ CONVERGES.

Abel's Test

The companion to Dirichlet's Test. Where Dirichlet requires $a_n\to 0$, Abel only requires $a_n$ to be monotone and bounded — and compensates by requiring $\sum b_n$ to converge outright.

I.
Abel's Test — Formal Statement
Stronger on $a_n$ requirements, weaker on $b_n$
Theorem (Abel)

Suppose both of the following hold:

  • ①$\{a_n\}$ is monotone and bounded: $a_n$ is eventually monotone (either non-increasing or non-decreasing) and $|a_n|\leq M$ for some constant $M$. Note: $a_n$ need not tend to zero.
  • ②$\sum b_n$ converges.

Then $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_n b_n$ converges.

Dirichlet vs. Abel — Side-by-Side
ConditionDirichletAbel
$a_n$ behaviorMust $\to 0$ monotonicallyMonotone & bounded (may stabilize)
$b_n$ behaviorPartial sums bounded$\sum b_n$ must converge
Conclusion$\sum a_n b_n$ converges$\sum a_n b_n$ converges
Key Insight

Abel's Test handles cases where $a_n$ converges to a nonzero limit (like $a_n=(n+1)/n\to 1$). In this case, Dirichlet's Test cannot apply. If you then know $\sum b_n$ converges — Abel gives you the product convergence for free.

Φ
Abel's Test Protocol
Four phases — from product to conclusion
Phase 1 — Identify the Product

Write the summand as $a_n b_n$, where $\{a_n\}$ is the slowly-varying (possibly convergent) factor and $\{b_n\}$ is the oscillating or summable factor.

Phase 2 — Verify $\{a_n\}$ is Monotone and Bounded

Show $a_n$ is eventually non-increasing or non-decreasing, and $|a_n|\leq M$ for all $n$. Both must hold.

Phase 3 — Verify $\sum b_n$ Converges

Use any applicable test: AST, Dirichlet's, $p$-series, geometric, etc. This is the key expenditure of effort.

Phase 4 — Conclude

Both conditions verified → $\sum a_n b_n$ CONVERGES.

Worked Examples
Cases where $a_n$ does not decay to zero
1
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n+1}{n}\cdot\frac{(-1)^n}{n}$
Decompose

$a_n=\frac{n+1}{n}=1+\frac{1}{n}$. $b_n=\frac{(-1)^n}{n}$.

Verify $a_n$

$a_n$ is decreasing ($a_{n+1}<a_n$ since $1+1/(n+1)<1+1/n$) and bounded: $1<a_n\leq 2$. ✓

Verify $\sum b_n$

$\sum\frac{(-1)^n}{n}$ converges by the Alternating Series Test ($b_n=1/n\to 0$ monotonically). ✓

Conclusion

CONVERGES by Abel's Test.

2
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{2n}{2n+1}\cdot\frac{(-1)^n}{\sqrt{n}}$
Decompose

$a_n=\frac{2n}{2n+1}$ (increasing, bounded above by 1). $b_n=\frac{(-1)^n}{\sqrt{n}}$.

Verify $\sum b_n$

$\sum\frac{(-1)^n}{\sqrt{n}}$ converges by AST: $1/\sqrt{n}\to 0$ non-increasing. ✓

Conclusion

CONVERGES by Abel's Test.

3
Why Abel's Fails Without Monotonicity
Counterexample to Dropping Monotonicity

If $a_n$ merely bounded but not monotone, the conclusion can fail. Consider $a_n=\sin n$ (bounded by 1 but non-monotone) and $b_n=(-1)^n/n$ (converges by AST). The product $\sum\sin(n)(-1)^n/n$ can be shown to still converge, but this requires Dirichlet's Test — Abel's does not apply since $\sin n$ is not monotone.

Abel's Test  ·  Benjamin Suchoff
Monotone bounded $a_n$ + convergent $\sum b_n$ → $\sum a_n b_n$ CONVERGES.

CALCULUS II — ADVANCED SERIES TESTS

Kummer's Test — The Master Ratio Test

A single meta-theorem that generates the Ratio Test, Raabe's Test, Bertrand's Test, and infinitely many others by choosing the auxiliary sequence $\{c_n\}$. The most powerful elementary test in analysis.

I.
Kummer's Theorem
The unifying master test
Theorem (Kummer, 1835)

Let $\sum a_n$ be a series of positive terms and $\{c_n\}$ be a sequence of positive constants. Define:

$$K=\lim_{n\to\infty}\left(c_n\frac{a_n}{a_{n+1}}-c_{n+1}\right)$$
$K$ valueVerdict
$K>0$CONVERGES (absolutely)
$K<0$ or $K=-\infty$DIVERGES (provided $\sum 1/c_n$ diverges)
$K=0$INCONCLUSIVE — try larger $c_n$
The Family of Tests — Special Cases of Kummer's
Choose $c_n$Recovered TestLimit Form
$c_n=1$D'Alembert (Ratio) Test$K=\frac{1}{L}-1$ where $L=\lim\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}$
$c_n=n$Raabe's Test$K=\lim n\!\left(\frac{a_n}{a_{n+1}}-1\right)$
$c_n=n\ln n$Bertrand's Test$K=\lim n\ln n\!\left(\frac{a_n}{a_{n+1}}-1\right)-1$

When a simpler $c_n$ gives $K=0$ (inconclusive), upgrade to the next level of the hierarchy.

Kummer's Test Protocol
Five phases — auxiliary sequence to verdict
Phase 1 — Positivity Check

Confirm $a_n>0$ for all $n$ beyond some point. Kummer's Test requires positive series.

Phase 2 — Choose $\{c_n\}$

Start with the simplest: $c_n=1$. If inconclusive, escalate to $c_n=n$, then $c_n=n\ln n$, etc.

Phase 3 — Compute $K$
$$K=\lim_{n\to\infty}\left(c_n\frac{a_n}{a_{n+1}}-c_{n+1}\right)$$

Simplify the ratio $a_n/a_{n+1}$ first, then substitute $c_n$ and $c_{n+1}$.

Phase 4 — Classify

$K>0$: converges. $K<0$: diverges (confirm $\sum 1/c_n$ diverges for the chosen $c_n$). $K=0$: escalate.

Worked Examples
From Ratio Test to Raabe's — the escalation in action
1
$\displaystyle\sum\frac{1}{n^2}$ via Raabe's ($c_n=n$)
Compute Ratio
$$\frac{a_n}{a_{n+1}}=\frac{(n+1)^2}{n^2}$$
Kummer Expression
$$K=\lim_{n\to\infty}\left(n\cdot\frac{(n+1)^2}{n^2}-(n+1)\right)=\lim\frac{(n+1)^2}{n}-(n+1)=\lim\frac{(n+1)^2-n(n+1)}{n}=\lim\frac{n+1}{n}=1>0$$
Conclusion

$K=1>0$ → CONVERGES. ✓ (consistent with $p=2$)

2
$\displaystyle\sum\frac{(2n)!}{4^n(n!)^2}$ — Where Ratio Test Fails
Ratio Test Attempt ($c_n=1$)
$$\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}=\frac{(2n+2)!}{4^{n+1}((n+1)!)^2}\cdot\frac{4^n(n!)^2}{(2n)!}=\frac{(2n+2)(2n+1)}{4(n+1)^2}=\frac{4n^2+6n+2}{4n^2+8n+4}\to 1$$

$L=1$: Ratio Test is inconclusive. Escalate to $c_n=n$.

Raabe's Test ($c_n=n$)
$$K=\lim n\!\left(\frac{a_n}{a_{n+1}}-1\right)=\lim n\left(\frac{4n^2+8n+4}{4n^2+6n+2}-1\right)=\lim n\cdot\frac{2n+2}{4n^2+6n+2}=\lim\frac{2n^2+2n}{4n^2+6n+2}=\frac{1}{2}$$
Apply Kummer's

$K=1/2>0$ → converges. But wait — is $1/2>0$? Yes. So the series converges. However, this is a borderline case: $K=1/2$ is the Raabe limit. Since $K=1/2>0$, Kummer's verdict is convergence.

Conclusion

$K=1/2>0$ → CONVERGES. Ratio Test failed; Raabe's (via Kummer's) succeeded.

3
$\displaystyle\sum\frac{1}{n}$ — Exposing the Limit of Raabe's
Raabe's Computation ($c_n=n$)
$$K=\lim n\!\left(\frac{n+1}{n}-1\right)=\lim n\cdot\frac{1}{n}=1$$

Wait — actually $K=\lim(n\cdot a_n/a_{n+1}-(n+1))$. With $a_n=1/n$: $a_n/a_{n+1}=(n+1)/n$.

$$K=\lim\!\left(n\cdot\frac{n+1}{n}-(n+1)\right)=\lim\big((n+1)-(n+1)\big)=0$$
Inconclusive — Escalate

$K=0$ for $c_n=n$: Raabe's is inconclusive for $\sum 1/n$. Must use $c_n=n\ln n$ (Bertrand's Test). This is expected — $\sum 1/n$ is the boundary case of the $p$-series hierarchy.

Conclusion

Raabe's: INCONCLUSIVE. Bertrand's Test or Cauchy Condensation confirms DIVERGES.

I.
Conversion Formulas — Linearization
The fundamental bridge between polar and rectangular
Polar → Rectangular (Linearization)

Given $(r,\theta)$, convert to $(x,y)$:

$$x=r\cos\theta \qquad y=r\sin\theta \qquad r^2=x^2+y^2 \qquad \tan\theta=\frac{y}{x}$$
Linearization Protocol — Eliminate $r$ and $\theta$

To convert a polar equation $r=f(\theta)$ to rectangular form:

  • ①Multiply both sides by $r$ if needed to create $r^2=x^2+y^2$.
  • ②Replace $r\cos\theta\to x$, $r\sin\theta\to y$, $r^2\to x^2+y^2$.
  • ③Complete the square or collect terms to identify the conic.

IV.
Slope of Tangent Lines in Polar
Differentiating polar curves
Derivation

Since $x=r\cos\theta$ and $y=r\sin\theta$, the slope $dy/dx$ is obtained via the chain rule:

$$\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{dy/d\theta}{dx/d\theta}=\frac{\dfrac{dr}{d\theta}\sin\theta+r\cos\theta}{\dfrac{dr}{d\theta}\cos\theta-r\sin\theta}$$
Horizontal and Vertical Tangents
  • HHorizontal tangent: $dy/d\theta=0$ and $dx/d\theta\neq 0$. Find $\theta$ where numerator is zero.
  • VVertical tangent: $dx/d\theta=0$ and $dy/d\theta\neq 0$. Find $\theta$ where denominator is zero.
Example — Tangent to Cardioid $r=1+\cos\theta$ at $\theta=\pi/2$
$$\frac{dr}{d\theta}=-\sin\theta\bigg|_{\theta=\pi/2}=-1 \qquad r\bigg|_{\theta=\pi/2}=1+0=1$$
$$\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{(-1)(1)+1(0)}{(-1)(0)-1(1)}=\frac{-1}{-1}=1$$

The tangent line at $(r,\theta)=(1,\pi/2)$, i.e., $(0,1)$ in rectangular, has slope $1$.

V.
Area Enclosed by Polar Curves
The sector-area formula
Area Formula

The area of the region swept by $r=f(\theta)$ from $\theta=\alpha$ to $\theta=\beta$:

$$A=\frac{1}{2}\int_{\alpha}^{\beta}[r(\theta)]^2\,d\theta$$

This arises because each infinitesimal sector has area $\frac{1}{2}r^2\,d\theta$ (like a circular sector $\frac{1}{2}r^2\Delta\theta$).

Example — Area Inside Cardioid $r=1+\cos\theta$
$$A=\frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}(1+\cos\theta)^2\,d\theta=\frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}(1+2\cos\theta+\cos^2\theta)\,d\theta$$
$$=\frac{1}{2}\left[2\pi+0+\pi\right]=\frac{3\pi}{2}$$
Area Between Two Polar Curves
$$A=\frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta\left([r_{\text{outer}}]^2-[r_{\text{inner}}]^2\right)d\theta$$

Find intersections first by setting $r_1(\theta)=r_2(\theta)$, then integrate on the appropriate interval.
